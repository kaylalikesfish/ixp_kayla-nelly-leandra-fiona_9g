Materials and Magnets

Magnets are basically any object that can attract other metals. Materials include steel, iron, nickel, cobalt, etc. Magnets, unlike electricity, when split, retain two poles, North and South, while atoms can be only protons or electrons. Opposites attract, same poles repel.

Induced Magnetism

Induced magnets are normal metal objects that become magnets because of a stronger magnet. If the big magnet is North, the part of the induced magnet that sticks to the big magnet will be South. Its other end will be North, and the cycle will continue (if you add more metals).

Magnetic Field

A magnetic field is an invisible region of force surrounding magnets, electric currents, or moving charged particles, characterized by both strength and direction (a vector field). It exerts a magnetic force on ferromagnetic materials (like iron) and other magnets, typically visualized as lines running from the North to the South pole.

Methods of Magnetization and Demagnetization

A common form of magnetization is rubbing a strong magnet in the same direction multiple times to attract the opposite pole in the direction of the rubbing, therefore magnetizing it. Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism is a fundamental physical force describing the interaction between electrically charged particles. Moving electrons create magnetic fields, and changing magnetic fields can produce electric currents.

Magnetic Field in a Wire with Electric Current

An electric current flowing through a wire creates a circular magnetic field around it. The strength increases with higher current and decreases with distance. The direction is found using the right-hand rule.
